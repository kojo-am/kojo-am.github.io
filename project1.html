There is a version of this project that would have been straightforward to write. Build a model, get a reasonable accuracy score, present it as evidence that school outcomes are predictable, and move on. This is not that version.

What the analysis below shows is more interesting and more honest than a high-performing model would have been. It shows how much of what determines a school's results is invisible to public data, how easily a model can appear more powerful than it genuinely is depending on what variables it is given, and why the gap between what a model sees and what actually matters is the most important result in the whole project.

The question

Can we predict a school's KS2 attainment outcomes using publicly available data? And if we can, what does that tell us — and what does it not tell us — about what is actually happening in those schools?

All data used in this analysis comes from publicly available Department for Education publications. KS2 school performance tables covering 2022 to 2024, school-level absence data for the same period, school funding allocation data, and school characteristics data were cleaned, merged on school URN and year where appropriate, and combined into a working dataset of 43,513 school-year records after removing suppressed data and extreme edge cases.

Why Model 5 is the most honest result

Models 3 and 4 achieved R² scores of 0.36 and 0.38 respectively. That looks reasonable. But those models included variables such as prior attainment funding per pupil — funding linked to a school's historical attainment profile.

In other words, the model was partly using a variable that already carried information about prior performance to predict current performance. That is a form of circular reasoning. The model was not simply discovering that prior attainment matters. It was being handed a proxy for prior attainment and unsurprisingly finding it useful.

Removing those circular variables dropped the R² from 0.38 to 0.24. That gap is not a failure of the clean model. It is a demonstration of how easily a model can appear more powerful than it actually is.

Model 5 uses cleaner variables that do not directly encode prior attainment: absence rate, persistent absence rate, pupil premium proportion, school size, school category, and education phase. With these genuinely independent variables, the model explains 24% of the variation in KS2 outcomes. The remaining 76% is not explained by the public variables used in this clean model.

Three things the feature importance is telling us

Absence is the dominant signal

Absence rate and persistent absence rate together account for over half of the model's predictive power. That is striking, but it is not simply saying that pupils who miss school do worse. At school level, it suggests that a school's overall absence profile is one of the strongest available public signals of its attainment outcomes.

This does not mean that schools with high absence are failing. It may mean they are serving communities with higher deprivation, significant health needs, housing instability, or other factors that drive both absence and attainment patterns simultaneously. The model cannot tell the difference.

School type adds little predictive power

Whether a school is a community school, an academy, a free school, or a voluntary aided school adds very little predictive signal once absence, deprivation and school size are already included. That does not settle wider debates about school structures, but it does suggest that school type alone is a weak explanation for KS2 attainment differences in this model.

Deprivation and size carry substantial weight

Pupil premium proportion and school size each carry substantial predictive weight. The school size finding matters because small schools are inherently more volatile. A cohort of fifteen pupils where three are persistently absent looks very different statistically from a school of three hundred pupils with the same rate. A model can struggle to represent that human and statistical context properly.

The prediction gap matters
The clean model is useful for understanding broad patterns, but it is not accurate enough to judge individual schools.

What 76% unexplained variation means

Even the clean model explains only 24% of the variation in KS2 outcomes across the school-year records used in this analysis. The remaining 76% is not explained by the variables included in the clean model.

That is not a methodological failure. It is an accurate reflection of how complex school performance actually is. The things that most powerfully shape what happens in a school — the quality of teaching, the consistency of leadership over time, what a particular cohort experienced in their early years, relationships between teachers and families, local community pressures, pupil mobility, and many forms of need — are not captured well in public datasets. Some may not be fully capturable in data at all.

A model built on what can be measured will always miss some of what matters. This is not an argument against data. It is an argument for understanding what data can and cannot see.

What this model should never be used to do. A model that explains 24% of outcome variation and is on average around 10 percentage points from the actual result should not be used to make judgements about individual schools. It should not flag schools for intervention, rank schools, or draw conclusions about teaching quality. It should not be used to predict individual pupil outcomes. The purpose of building it is to understand what the available data can and cannot tell us — not to act on its outputs as if they were ground truth.
