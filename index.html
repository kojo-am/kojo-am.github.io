Education Article Series

A four-part series exploring what school assessment data in England can reveal, what it can hide, and why careful interpretation matters.

Written by Emmanuel Adu Amoako, this project brings together interests in data interpretation, responsible use of evidence, and better decision-making. It is designed as a public-facing series for teachers, school leaders, researchers, and anyone interested in how education data is used and understood.

Available Articles

  • Article 1: What school assessment data can show — and what it cannot An introduction to the limits of reading assessment data as self-explanatory, using Newham and England as an illustrative example.
  • Article 2: Can Data Science Support Earlier Identification of Learning Needs in Primary Classrooms? Coming soon — exploring how combining multiple indicators may support earlier and more careful identification of need.
  • Article 3: Why Teacher Professional Judgement Still Matters in Data-Informed Education Coming soon — examining why context, interpretation and classroom knowledge remain essential.
  • Article 4: What Ethical Education Technology Should Look Like in Primary Schools Coming soon — considering inclusion, fairness and responsible design in primary education technology.

About the Series

The purpose of this series is to encourage a more careful reading of school assessment data. Rather than treating attendance, attainment or pupil profile data as complete explanations, the articles explore how these indicators become more meaningful when interpreted together and supported by professional knowledge.

Across the series, the focus is not only on what data shows, but also on how it should be interpreted, where its limits lie and how more responsible data-informed systems might be designed in the future.

The series is intended for teachers, school leaders, education researchers and anyone interested in how data is used in public discussion about schools.

About the Author

Emmanuel Adu Amoako is a data scientist interested in how data, predictive analysis, and intelligent systems can be used responsibly to support better decisions. His work focuses on careful interpretation, practical application and the ethical use of data-driven tools in education.
