Data Science Project — Project 2

The Geography of Educational Inequality
Across England

A multi-indicator analysis of KS2 attainment, disadvantage gaps, absence, SEND and EAL outcomes across 151 local authorities in England — and what the patterns reveal about where data misleads.

This project started with a case that had already appeared in Article 1 of this series. Newham, a London borough with some of the highest deprivation and absence rates in England, consistently produces some of the strongest KS2 outcomes in the country. The purpose of this analysis was to build the dataset that would place that finding in full national context — and to see what other patterns emerged when five dimensions of educational inequality were mapped simultaneously across all 151 local authorities.

All data is drawn from the Department for Education's published statistics for the academic year 2024 to 2025. National averages throughout are weighted by pupil numbers rather than simple LA averages, which gives a more accurate picture of the national position for pupils than treating a small rural authority and a large urban one as equivalent.

The Newham paradox, confirmed at scale

When you map 151 local authorities across five dimensions simultaneously, most patterns are broadly predictable. Higher deprivation tends to produce lower overall attainment and larger disadvantage gaps. Higher absence rates tend to appear alongside other markers of social difficulty. The geography of inequality, once you can see it, follows recognisable contours.

Newham does not fit any of those contours. It is the single most statistically striking local authority in this dataset, and understanding why it sits where it does is the most important analytical question the data raises.

LA Spotlight

Newham — the case that breaks the model

Newham ranks 1st in England for disadvantaged pupil attainment at KS2, with 73% of its disadvantaged pupils meeting expected standard. The national average for disadvantaged pupils is 47.7%. Newham's disadvantaged pupils outperform the national average for all pupils combined. At the same time, Newham ranks 4th worst in England for persistent absence at 18.3%, and serves one of the most deprived and diverse populations in the country.

No predictive model would generate this combination. No league table would reveal it. It only becomes visible when you look across multiple indicators at once — which is the core argument of this entire series.

Why the absence-attainment relationship is weaker than expected

At school level, as shown in Project 1, absence is the strongest single predictor of KS2 outcomes. At local authority level, the relationship almost disappears — the correlation between persistent absence and overall attainment across these 151 LAs is close to zero. This is not a contradiction. It reflects a statistical phenomenon called the ecological fallacy: a relationship that holds at individual or school level can reverse or vanish when you aggregate to a higher level.

In this case, the explanation is straightforward. LAs with the highest absence rates are predominantly deprived urban authorities serving communities with complex social needs. Those same communities often also have strong school systems, high expectations, and targeted support infrastructure developed precisely because the challenges are well understood. The absence reflects the population. The attainment reflects the schools. The two do not move together at LA level because they are responding to different forces.

The disadvantage gap — where it is widest and why that matters

Across 151 local authorities, the average disadvantage attainment gap — the difference in KS2 outcomes between disadvantaged pupils and their peers — is 16.2 percentage points when weighted by pupil numbers. The range runs from 3 percentage points at the low end, which is Newham, to 30 percentage points at the high end, which is Wokingham.

Overall KS2 attainment versus disadvantage attainment gap
Higher-attaining LAs tend to have larger disadvantage gaps — not smaller ones. Hover over any point for details. This relationship challenges the assumption that strong overall performance means strong performance for all pupils.

The scatter above reveals something counterintuitive. There is a positive correlation between overall attainment and the size of the disadvantage gap — meaning that LAs with higher overall KS2 outcomes tend to have larger gaps between their disadvantaged pupils and the rest. Wokingham, Richmond upon Thames, York, and Bath and North East Somerset all appear in the top ten for the largest disadvantage gaps, yet all have above-average overall attainment.

The explanation is that in high-attaining, relatively affluent LAs, non-disadvantaged pupils perform very well, which mechanically increases the gap even if disadvantaged pupils are also performing reasonably. In deprived urban LAs, both groups face similar pressures and both tend to perform closer together — but at a lower overall level. The gap metric, taken in isolation, can therefore mislead: a small gap in a low-attaining LA is not the same as a small gap in a high-attaining one.

The strongest counterintuitive correlation in the dataset is between persistent absence and the disadvantage attainment gap, which is approximately -0.68. At first glance, this appears wrong: one might expect areas with higher absence to have larger disadvantage gaps. But the pattern reflects how deprivation operates at local authority level. In many deprived urban authorities, absence is high for both disadvantaged and non-disadvantaged pupils, which compresses the measured gap between the two groups. By contrast, in more affluent authorities, non-disadvantaged pupils often perform especially strongly, making the gap appear larger even when disadvantaged pupils are not necessarily performing very poorly. The negative correlation therefore does not mean absence is harmless; it means the gap measure is shaped by both sides of the comparison.

Newham has a disadvantage gap of 3 percentage points. Its disadvantaged pupils achieve 73% — which is 10 percentage points above the national average for all pupils. That is not a small gap at a low level. That is a small gap at a high level, and it is the most unusual combination in the dataset.

The EAL finding — what the data says and what it does not

Nationally, EAL pupils achieve an average of 66.2% at KS2 when weighted by pupil numbers — 3.6 percentage points above the national average for all pupils. This challenges the widespread assumption that EAL status is a straightforward risk factor for attainment. At LA level, the picture is complex. The EAL attainment difference ranges from -18 percentage points in Redcar and Cleveland, where EAL pupils significantly underperform the local average, to +12 percentage points in the Isle of Wight and Wokingham, where EAL pupils substantially outperform it.

What this range suggests is that the relationship between EAL status and attainment is not fixed. It depends heavily on the specific linguistic communities involved, the infrastructure of EAL support in a given LA, and whether EAL pupils are concentrated in schools with strong track records of language development. The aggregate national figure hides enormous local variation, and that variation is precisely what a single national measure cannot capture.

The SEND gap — nationally consistent, locally invisible

The SEND attainment gap is the most consistent finding in the dataset, which is itself a significant result. Across 151 local authorities, the average gap between SEND pupils and their peers is 38.6 percentage points. The standard deviation is only 3.2 percentage points — meaning that almost every LA in England has a SEND gap of between 35 and 42 points. The map of SEND gaps looks almost uniform because the reality almost is uniform.

This is not a geography of opportunity story for SEND pupils. It is a national systemic story. The gap does not vary meaningfully by region, by deprivation level, or by overall LA attainment. It is present everywhere at roughly the same magnitude. That finding is more troubling than a map showing high variation would be — because variation at least implies that somewhere is doing it differently. Near-uniformity implies that the system itself is producing a consistent outcome, and that the levers for change may not be at LA level at all.

What this analysis cannot tell you

Working at local authority level imposes significant constraints on what can and cannot be said. Each LA average conceals enormous variation between schools within it. A high-attaining LA may contain schools that are significantly underperforming, and a low-attaining LA may contain schools that are doing remarkable work with difficult circumstances. The aggregate figure does not tell you which schools those are.

The absence and attainment figures also come from the same year, which means the analysis is cross-sectional rather than longitudinal. It shows the state of the system in 2024/25 but cannot establish whether changes in absence in previous years are driving current attainment outcomes, or vice versa. Establishing causal direction requires a different kind of analysis.

Finally, and most importantly, this analysis should not be used to rank local authorities, judge their leadership, or draw conclusions about which areas deserve more or less support. The patterns it reveals are the starting point for questions, not the basis for decisions. That is the same conclusion Project 1 reached about school-level modelling, and it applies with equal force here.

What this analysis should not be used to do. Local authority averages mask school-level variation and should not be used to make judgements about individual schools, headteachers, or communities. The data is cross-sectional and does not establish causation. The purpose of this analysis is to make patterns visible and to prompt better questions — not to provide a basis for policy decisions or resource allocation without substantial further investigation.
