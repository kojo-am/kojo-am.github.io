What SATs Scores Miss

On pressure, narrowing, setting, and the limits of reducing children to a score.

← Back to homepage

Written by: Kojo Amoako

Published: 20 April 2026

I am sitting at the back of a Year 6 classroom in East London, watching thirty children prepare for one of the most consequential weeks of their young lives. The timetable on the wall tells the story without needing any explanation. Maths. English. Maths. English. Reading comprehension. Grammar. Repeat. The art display in the corner has not been updated since February. The science equipment sits in a cupboard. There is a reason for all of this, and it is not complicated: SATs results feed into school performance tables, performance tables shape a school's reputation, and reputation influences how many parents choose to send their children there. More children means more funding. The pressure on headteachers is not imagined. It is structural, it is real, and it flows directly down into the classroom timetable these children sit in front of every day.

But it is the boy in the corner that I keep coming back to.

He is not disruptive. He is not behind. He is sitting quietly, doing what he has been asked to do. But every so often something happens and a teacher mentions the weather, or light, or why plants grow toward windows, and his whole face changes. His hand goes up before the sentence is finished. Last week he wanted to know why the sky is blue during the day but orange at sunset. The week before that he asked whether plants can feel pain when their leaves are cut. This week, unprompted, he turned to the child next to him and said: if space is a vacuum, why can we hear sounds in science videos from space? He is ten years old. He notices things. He wants to understand the world.

In May, he will sit tests in reading, grammar, punctuation and spelling, and mathematics. Those results will travel with him into secondary school as part of the information his new school receives. They will say something about his attainment in a small number of core areas. They will say nothing about the mind that wanted to know why the sky changes colour. That child is not below average. The test is.

This is not an isolated case. Across Year 6 classrooms in England every spring, children who are curious, creative, capable and genuinely motivated are being narrowed by a system that was never designed to see them whole. The KS2 SATs measure attainment in a limited set of domains, under timed conditions, in a single week, at age ten or eleven. What they offer is a snapshot. What they do not offer is a full account of the child.

Something many parents do not realise is that KS2 SATs results do not determine which secondary school a child attends. Year 7 places are allocated through published admissions criteria such as catchment area, siblings, distance, and faith criteria in some schools. National Offer Day falls in early March, two full months before SATs are even sat. The results cannot be used to decide where a child goes next. The stress is real. But the meaning attached to those scores is often far larger than the admissions reality.

What the score can influence, however, is something subtler and in some ways more damaging. When children arrive in Year 7, many secondary schools use prior attainment data alongside their own baseline assessments to decide how pupils are grouped. A major study of over nine thousand Year 7 pupils across forty-six secondary schools in England found that nearly a third had been placed into the wrong set relative to what their actual results warranted. More strikingly, Black students were two and a half times more likely to be placed into lower sets than their prior attainment justified. The test is partial. The systems built on top of it can be more partial still.

The mental and emotional toll of all this is not abstract. The National Education Union has documented widespread increases in test related anxiety among primary pupils in the weeks leading up to SATs, with children experiencing sleep disruption, loss of appetite and tearfulness. These are ten and eleven year olds. They are not anxious because they are fragile. They are anxious because the adults around them, under enormous institutional pressure, cannot always hide the weight these results carry. Children feel what is at stake. They always do.

What troubles me most is not the pressure alone. It is what the pressure is in service of. SATs are often treated as though they tell us something large and lasting about a child's academic ability. In reality, they tell us something narrower: how a child performed in a specific set of tasks, in a specific format, on specific days, across a small number of subject areas. That information has value. But it cannot bear the weight that adults sometimes place on it.

That matters even more now, in a moment when our understanding of intelligence ought to be changing. We live in a world where artificial intelligence can write essays, solve structured problems and generate code in seconds. If machines can replicate procedural correctness at scale, then the qualities that matter most in human learning may not be the ones most easily captured by a standardised test. Curiosity. Creativity. Judgement. The ability to notice, connect, doubt and wonder. Howard Gardner spent decades arguing that human intelligence is not one thing but many, and that schools consistently reward only a narrow version of it. SATs are one of the clearest examples of that narrowing in action.

The boy in the corner has a kind of scientific curiosity that is genuinely rare. In a different sort of week, that quality would be visible and valued. In SATs week, it is invisible. He will be measured on whether he can identify grammatical structures and solve arithmetic problems. Both are useful skills. Neither of them is why his eyes light up.

What might have helped us see him more clearly has been quietly pushed aside. Art. Music. Extended science. Practical inquiry. These are the parts of school through which children often reveal who they are and how they think. For many late bloomers, many children with English as an additional language, and many whose strengths are practical or creative, those subjects tell us more about future potential than a test score ever could. When a child arrives in Year 7 already carrying a label from a system that could not see them whole, they begin to adjust their expectations of themselves accordingly. Passion does not survive that adjustment easily. By the time they are free to choose their own subjects, the choice may already have been made for them.

There is a version of accountability in schools that makes sense. Knowing whether children are learning to read, write and reason matters, and no serious critic of SATs argues that assessment itself should disappear. But a single high-stakes test, sat in one week, generating a score that does not decide secondary admission yet still shapes how children are grouped and seen the moment they arrive. Is that a fair or proportionate instrument? It asks a great deal of children in exchange for a measure far narrower than the stories later told about it.

The next time a child comes home with a SATs result, look past the number. Ask what it measured. Ask what it missed. And ask what kind of system reduces a child who wants to know why the sky turns orange at sunset to a score, and then calls that score a verdict.
